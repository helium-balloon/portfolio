Iterative Design: Warp Home

UI/UX design for a startup homepage

Overview

For this project, my team and I worked with Warp to design a home page based on their project brief. This was an amazing opportunity to learn about designing with a team and complete the iterative design process (sketches, low fidelity wireframe, and high fidelity prototypes). We also received feedback on our wireframe from both students in our class, a Senior Product Designer at Warp, and a Design Executive at Google Ventures in order to improve our design.

About the Product

Warp is a customizable terminal with collaborative tools and AI integration. Warp does not have a centralized home page for users to view information about their files, team, usage, account and learn more about Warp. Currently, a lot of this information is in the settings page, which makes it difficult to find. The home page that we designed will serve as an aggregator that ultimately allows for increased discovery and usage of Warp’s features.

Process

Research and Sketching

To begin this project, we first delved into understanding the project brief, exploring prior work, and researching Warp to understand the product. We then each created at least 8 sketches to explore different layouts for the screen and modules (components on the screen) as well as layouts for different user states (new user versus active user).

Our main goal with the home page was to create a user-friendly, glanceable interface that allows users to understand their current state and easily access their content. We achieved this by:

  • Merging the current drive on Warp into our new home pages to improve intuitive usability and reduce time spent searching for desired content.
  • Centralizing metrics, live sessions, activity, and Warp content on a left hand sidebar and aimed for visual appeal by using profile icons for team members, filled bars for metrics, and link icons to indicate that clicking on the Warp resources would redirect users.
  • Seamlessly switching between personal and team views with the toggle to organize content while still keeping team information accessible
  • Catering to both new users (requiring more instructional guidance) and active users (with their current content readily accessible).

Ultimately, our design aligns with the priorities outlined in our project brief and enhances the discovery of Warp’s features by moving key functionalities from the settings page to the home page.

Feedback on Wireframes

I filmed a five minute presentation of our wireframe using Loom to share with peers and our contact at Warp to gain feedback.

Below is some of the feedback that we received about our first iteration wireframe:

  • It is unclear how to get from the current view to the terminal and how to get from the terminal to this view.
  • You should ensure that the order of modules on the left navigation bar is based on what is most important to the user.
  • People may want to have more control over how they see the items in their drive. For instance, you could have an option to toggle between list and gallery mode, filters and sorting.

We incorporated most of the feedback in our second iteration. This includes:

  • Removing the metrics from the left sidebar since metrics was last on our priorities list, and it did not feel necessary for users to constantly see this information (better in the account page).
  • Adding basic drive features we missed: trash, search bar, sorting, filters, modes for viewing (list and grid)
  • Changing toggle (navigating between “Personal” and “Team”) to a drop-down so users can be on multiple teams.
  • Using the same top bar as the Warp app, but adding a new tab for the home page, to easily navigate between the home page and terminal.

Throughout this process, we emphasized meeting the user's need of being able to access all information in a central and easy to use location as well as creating an experience that is applicable to all users on the platform. We emphasized key design principles in our final prototype to fulfill to do this by:

  • Ensuring that our design aligns with the mental model users currently have of a homepage by making all information accessible from the page, making it the central location.
  • Using scan patterns to create a layout that is efficient for users through the F scan pattern.
  • Using hierarchical design to create a easy to understand layout.

At the end of this process, we presented our final design to Product Designers at Warp and received feecback.

Learnings

Through this project, I learned a lot about designing as a team and interacting with real clients in order to achieve a design goal. One of the key challenges we faced was translating Warp's vision into tangible designs. This required not only creative problem-solving but also a deep understanding of design principles and user experience considerations. We took their feedback and continuously iterated on our designs, constantly striving to align with their expectations while also incorporating our own expertise and insights. I'm so grateful for this experience to strength my design and communication skills, and I'm ready to utilize these skills moving forward.
